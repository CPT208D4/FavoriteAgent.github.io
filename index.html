What is Pockety Favorite Agent?

A playful AI system for cross-platform favorites digestion

Tired of endlessly saving tutorials, articles, and videos across Bilibili, RedNote, Douyin, and CSDN, only to never revisit your ever-growing collection?

Pockety is an AI-powered favorites management system built to cure modern Collection Digestion Disorder. It automatically syncs and categorizes cross-platform saved content, generates personalized weekly knowledge digests and audio summaries, builds an interactive visualized knowledge graph from scattered favorites, and lets users chat and ask questions directly over their own saved library with advanced RAG-powered AI.

Designed with a human-centric and playful experience, Pockety transforms idle "save and forget" clutter into an organized, actively growing personal knowledge base, making learning effortless and efficient.

The Spark: Why This Matters?

From excessive saving to poor digestion

In the current era of severe information overload, college students extensively consume fragmented content from WeChat, Douyin, RedNote, and Bilibili daily. They frequently save articles, videos, notes and resources to platform favorites with the positive expectation of reviewing, learning and digesting these materials later. Nevertheless, long-term observations and surveys among student groups reveal an extremely common issue: most saved favorite content is never reopened, read or learned. Instead, it gradually accumulates as useless digital garbage, forming a typical Collection-Digestion Disorder.

This gap between active collection and passive abandonment causes serious waste of information resources, weakens learning efficiency, and leads to fragmented, disorganized personal knowledge. Existing tools either focus on simple bookmark storage or lack playful automated digestion mechanisms. Therefore, our team chooses this user-centric intelligent knowledge management track. We aim to develop Pockety (FavoriteAgent) to unify cross-platform favorites, automate content organization, provide playful periodic digests, and build a queryable personal knowledge base, fundamentally solving Collection-Digestion Disorder and turning dormant collections into usable knowledge for college students.

Persona 01

Yu Chen

Age 20 · Female · University Student (Computer Science Major)

Typical Behavior

Watches tutorials on Bilibili daily, saves code on CSDN, and keeps notes on Xiaohongshu. Believes saving means learning, but revisit rate is below 10%.

Pain Points

  • Search Barriers: Must switch between four apps during exams/projects to find one remembered technical point.
  • Anxiety Loop: More than 500 unread items trigger stress and avoidance.
  • Logical Disconnect: Related Bilibili and CSDN content cannot be linked into one knowledge path.

Core Expectations

Connect cross-platform knowledge, auto-build a knowledge graph, and support Q&A from saved content.

Persona 02

Adam

Age 22 · Male · Senior Student / Job Seeker / New Intern

Typical Behavior

Saves resume/interview/internship/product resources in bursts. Adds dozens weekly but rarely revisits, so the collection becomes a mental comfort zone.

Pain Points

  • Asset Fragility: Valuable saved content is later deleted or paywalled, causing frustration and helplessness.
  • Categorization Fatigue: Manual tagging is tiring, but no tagging leads to chaos and a dumping-ground folder.
  • Lack of Reminders: Key deadlines are missed without proactive reminders, so plans and actions disconnect.

Core Expectations

Keep reliable backups even if source posts disappear, quickly extract key takeaways, and categorize/tag content for easier retrieval.

From Option A to Option B: The Shift We Made

Comparison between original and optimized direction

Option A (Original)

Rigid, high-saturation solid matcha green flat UI. Lacking depth or whitespace, all modules appear as monotonous, indistinguishable blocks. Weekly reports rely on rigid text lists - completely lacking any engaging visual interaction.

Option B (Optimized)

We adopted a soft, dreamy pastel gradient system with generous whitespace. Core functions are structured as independent, elevated floating cards. We introduced a playful cloud-shaped visual hierarchy for the weekly overview, turning abstract data into friendly, approachable graphics.

Justification

This iteration embodies a Playful design philosophy beyond aesthetics: The gentle, vibrant color palette and airy layout creates a stress-free environment, dissolving users' anxiety towards their cluttered collections. Playful cloud visualization transforms tedious data sorting into an intuitive, delightful experience. Floating cards establish clear visual boundaries, while the whimsical style aligns perfectly with our AI-powered dialogue and sorting features.

Evaluation & Reflection

Usability findings, iteration direction, and team reflections

This section summarizes usability findings, iteration direction, and final reflections from the team.

Real People, Real Clicks, Real Truth

Survey and interview feedback after high-fidelity prototype

  • After completing the high-fidelity prototype, we conducted a survey and short interviews with volunteers.
  • The results showed that the front page was the most confusing part, they reported difficulties in understanding how to use it and only one participant was aware of the function of this system after the first seen of this system.
  • Some users also criticized the design of the front page and the report page, and some did not notice that the favorites collection could be expanded without guidance. One of the users I interviewed told me that you should add more messages informing users about the ai function, otherwise they might overlook it.
  • In addition, another user informs me that the ai chatting is not smart enough, which might stick to the favorite knowledge without considering the meaning of the question. Also, an active question could be added at the end of each response, which connects and binds more information in the favorites. None of the participants selected the weekly report as the most valuable feature, suggesting that this function may need to be redesigned in later iterations.
  • Despite these drawbacks, most participants still expressed a willingness to use the system and recommend it to others

Break It, Fix It, Level Up

Improvement direction based on testing insights

In our evaluation, we conducted two rounds of user testing. The first round involved participants who had previously tested our low-fi prototype, while the second round involved first-time users of the current system.

The results indicated that the current version still has several usability issues, particularly in workflow guidance and homepage design. Among users who had tested the low-fi prototype, 25% reported concerns about the homepage design. However, among first-time users, this complaint rate increased to 35.7%. In terms of function entrances, almost all low-fi prototype users were already familiar with them, whereas only 70.5% of first-time users clearly understood the meaning of each function entrance. During the interviews, participants also pointed out that the auto-categorization function lacked transparency and user autonomy

To address this issue, we made one design improvement. When users upload a file, the system now displays a notification for five seconds, showing which collection the document has been automatically stored in. This change aims to make the auto-categorization process more transparent and easier for users to understand.

Despite these drawbacks, several functions received positive feedback from participants. Among users who had tested the low-fi prototype, 87.5% stated that they would like to continue using the system. In addition, most participants were clearly aware of the AI function and were able to use it smoothly. Overall, 95.5% of participants could interact with the system using ready-made questions, and 81% found it easy to chat with the AI assistant. The UI design also received positive comments from users, as shown in the pie chart.

If We Knew Then What We Know Now

What we learned from design and validation

Ethic and social impact

Data privacy is a key ethical concern in our system, as users are required to upload content and files. Since the auto-categorization function needs to analyse the uploaded materials, users should be clearly informed about how their data is processed, stored, and used. Accessing users' saved or favourite content should also require explicit user permission. Therefore, our design should reduce unnecessary data exposure and ensure that users understand how their content is used by the system.

From a social perspective, our design may help users reduce information overload caused by excessive content saving. However, users should not rely completely on AI-generated summaries or recommendations. The AI functions should support users' learning, searching, and reviewing processes rather than replace their own judgement.

AI use

We mainly use generative AI tools (such as Cursor and ChatGPT) as a development assistant to build a FastAPI back-end framework, connect SQLite and Chroma databases, and reconstruct the HTML exported by Figma into usable components. This greatly reduces the workload of the sample code and accelerates the prototype development. However, the code generated by AI is often only applicable to the "ideal path", and problems such as upload function, CORS cross-domain restrictions and httpx timeout still need to be carefully reviewed and debugged manually. In practice, AI is more like a draft generator than a reliable engineer, and the key links still need to be judged manually.

In general, AI has accelerated the implementation process but failed to replace decision-making. We still need to judge for ourselves when to adopt AI output, when to rewrite the code and how to deal with the failure scenario. The project clearly shows that while AI improves productivity, it also expands the boundaries of responsibility.
